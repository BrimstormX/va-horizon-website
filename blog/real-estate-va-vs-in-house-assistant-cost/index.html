Most wholesalers think they are comparing $6/hr to $18/hr when they evaluate a VA against an in-house hire. That is not the comparison. The real question is total annual cost including salary, benefits, payroll taxes, recruiting, ramp time, management overhead, and replacement cost versus a managed VA placement where the agency absorbs most of that friction.

When you run the full numbers, the cost difference is not marginal. It is 3-5x. And for outbound cold calling specifically, the VA also produces 3-4x the daily dial volume of a U.S.-based in-house caller because of Readymode access. The math works in every direction.

Important context: This comparison is specific to outbound cold calling roles for real estate wholesaling. In-house hiring can make sense for other functions (closings coordination, in-person acquisitions, local market relationships). For high-volume outbound dialing, the numbers below represent the actual cost structure.

1. Why This Comparison Matters for Wholesalers

The gut-check hiring decision in real estate wholesaling usually goes one of two ways. Either the operator hires someone local because "I want to be able to walk over to their desk," or they hire cheap offshore because "I just need someone to make calls." Both approaches fail for the same reason: they skip the math.

The operator who insists on in-house often spends $60,000-90,000/year for 100-300 dials per day from a caller who cannot use Readymode and requires 10-15 hours per week of their personal management time. The operator who hires cheap offshore often ends up with a general VA who has never worked a motivated seller script and produces zero qualified leads after 3 months.

The actual decision is: what does it cost to produce a qualified lead consistently, and what level of operational overhead am I taking on to get there? The numbers below answer that question with specific figures.

5. Ramp Time and Training Cost

In-house hiring does not produce output on day one. The timeline from job posting to first productive dial is typically 6-14 weeks:

  • Job posting and applications: 1-2 weeks
  • Screening, interviews, offer: 1-2 weeks
  • Notice period (if poaching from another job): 2 weeks
  • Script training and role play: 1-2 weeks
  • Supervised calls with daily feedback: 2-3 weeks

At an 8-week ramp at $3,750/month base salary, you are paying $7,500 before receiving a single productive dial. And during those 8 weeks, you are spending 10-20 hours of your own time writing scripts, running role plays, reviewing calls, and configuring tools.

VA Horizon agency placement: the VA is dialing a live list within 48-72 hours of engagement start. Script training, role play certification, CRM configuration, and dialer setup are completed before day one. Client time required during ramp: near zero.

The ramp cost reality: Eight weeks of paid in-house ramp time at $3,750/month is $7,500 in salary before the first call is made. Add 80 hours of your time at any reasonable value for your hours, and the real cost of the ramp period is $10,000-15,000. That covers 9-11 months of a VA Horizon engagement.

6. Management Overhead

Most wholesalers dramatically underestimate how much time an in-house hire requires. The list is longer than it looks:

  • Weekly performance review and feedback session
  • Script updates and retraining when market or list changes
  • CRM audits to verify accurate lead tagging
  • List pulling and skip tracing coordination
  • Tech support when the dialer or CRM has issues
  • Time-off coverage and schedule management
  • HR issues when they arise (performance improvement, conflicts, termination)
  • Motivation management during slow deal-flow periods

Conservative estimate: 8-12 hours per week of operator time in the first 90 days, settling to 4-6 hours per week ongoing. At any reasonable valuation of your time as a wholesaler, this is $2,000-4,000/month in opportunity cost.

With a VA Horizon managed engagement: VA Horizon handles list sourcing, skip tracing, CRM tagging, weekly QA, call review, and performance management. Client time is 1-2 hours per week reviewing a dashboard or weekly performance report. The agency handles the operational work.

7. Replacement Cost

U.S. sales employee turnover runs 30-40% per year. When a cold caller leaves, replacement cost is 50-150% of their annual salary. For a $45,000 employee:

  • Recruiting cost (job boards, time, interviews): $2,000-5,000
  • Productivity loss during open role: 4-8 weeks at full salary value = $3,500-7,000
  • Ramp cost for replacement: $7,500-15,000 (same calculation as above)
  • Total replacement cost per event: $13,000-27,000

At 30% annual turnover, you should expect a replacement event every 3-4 years at minimum. Amortized annually, that is $3,250-6,750 per year in expected replacement cost added to your base employee cost.

VA Horizon agency replacement policy: if a VA underperforms or leaves, VA Horizon replaces them at no additional cost within the engagement. Downtime is measured in days, not months. No additional recruiting cost. No ramp period on your dime.

With an in-house hire, performance accountability is entirely your problem. If the cold caller is producing 300 dials/day when the standard is 800, you need to investigate, document, counsel, manage a performance improvement plan, and ultimately make a termination decision if it does not improve. This takes 2-4 months of reduced output plus your time managing the HR process.

VA Horizon guarantees 30 qualified leads per month minimum per cold calling engagement. If that target is not met, VA Horizon continues dialing at no additional charge until the target is reached, or places additional VAs to hit the target within the original timeframe. The accountability sits with the agency, not with you.

VAs also dial with Readymode's predictive algorithm logging every call. Dial counts, contact rates, and lead submission numbers are tracked automatically. There is no ambiguity about whether 800 dials happened or 300 dials happened. The data is in the system.

10. When Each Option Makes Sense

The math heavily favors a managed VA for outbound cold calling. But in-house hiring is the right call for certain situations. Be honest about which situation you are in.

In-House Makes Sense When

  • You need someone physically present for in-person acquisitions, property walkthroughs, or local relationship management.
  • Your operation involves complex judgment calls that require real-time discussion in the same room.
  • You are building a team where long-term career growth and company culture are priorities.
  • You are at sufficient volume (20+ deals/year) that building internal institutional knowledge is strategically valuable.

VA Makes Sense When

  • Your primary need is high-volume outbound cold calling to generate qualified leads.
  • You want pipeline production without the operational overhead of hiring, training, and managing a U.S. employee.
  • You are not yet at a volume that justifies a full-time in-house team.
  • You want guaranteed output with a replacement guarantee and zero recruiting exposure.
  • You want Readymode access without buying 3-5 seats independently.
The bottom line: For outbound cold calling in real estate wholesaling, a managed VA at $1,360/month all-in versus an in-house hire at $5,200-8,100/month all-in is a 3.8-6x cost difference. The VA also produces 3-4x the daily dial volume. For this specific role, the math is not close.
