Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Distressed supply is rising but still historically thin. Q1 2026 filings are up 26% year over year, yet at one in 1,211 housing units, foreclosures remain far below the 2010 crisis peak. Treat it as a growing trickle, not a flood.
  • ✓ Where to aim a list-pull right now: Indiana, South Carolina, Florida, Delaware, and Illinois carry the worst state foreclosure rates, while Texas, Florida, and California produce the highest raw volume of foreclosure starts. Rate tells you density, raw counts tell you how many doors to dial.
  • ✓ Foreclosure starts (82,631 in Q1, up 20% year over year) are the leading indicator for wholesalers. A start is a homeowner who just got served, which is the front of the motivated-seller pipeline, long before it becomes a bank-owned REO.
  • ✓ REOs jumping 45% year over year means more deals are completing the cycle, but bank-owned is a late, crowded stage. The earlier you reach the owner, the less competition and the more room to solve their problem.
  • ✓ Use these numbers to pick markets, not to time the whole country. A metro like Cleveland (1 in 1,524) or Tampa (1 in 1,878) can run roughly 2x the national monthly rate, so build your buybox around the hot pockets, not the headline.

Every few months a headline lands that says foreclosures are "surging," and every few months a wholesaler texts me asking if the wave is finally here. The honest answer for 2026 is: filings are climbing steadily, the year-over-year numbers look big, but the absolute level is still low. Both things are true at once, and if you only read the headline you will misread the market.

This is a data roundup, not a hot take. I pulled the latest ATTOM figures, lined them up by quarter, by state, and by metro, and framed all of it the way an investor actually uses it: as a proxy for where motivated-seller supply is growing. Distressed homeowners are the rawest motivated sellers there are, so the foreclosure map is a useful first cut at where to point cold outreach. Let me walk through it.

One accuracy note before the numbers: quarterly and monthly rates are not the same and never line up. The national rate is one in 1,211 housing units for the full quarter (Q1 2026) but one in 3,562 for a single month (May 2026). I label the period on every figure below, and you should too when you quote these.

1. 2026 Foreclosure Snapshot

Start with the top-line number. In Q1 2026, 118,727 U.S. properties had a foreclosure filing, which ATTOM defines as a default notice, a scheduled auction, or a bank repossession. That is up 6% from Q4 2025 and up 26% year over year, per ATTOM's Q1 2026 U.S. Foreclosure Market Report. The national foreclosure rate for the quarter was one in every 1,211 housing units.

Here is the full quarter at a glance, broken into the three stages that matter.

Metric (Q1 2026) Count vs Q4 2025 vs Q1 2025
Total properties with a filing 118,727 +6% +26%
Foreclosure starts (process initiated) 82,631 +7% +20%
Completed foreclosures (REO) 14,020 +2% +45%
National foreclosure rate 1 in 1,211 units n/a n/a

Read those three rows together and you see the shape of the pipeline. Starts (82,631, up 20% year over year) are the front end: homeowners who just entered the process. REOs (14,020, up 45% year over year) are the back end: properties the bank already took back. The starts number is the one a wholesaler should watch most, because it is the leading indicator of motivated sellers entering the market.

Filings are not "homes lost." A single property can pick up multiple filings as it moves through default, auction, and repossession. Starts and REOs are stages inside that total, not separate piles you add on top. So 118,727 filings does not mean 118,727 families losing homes in the quarter. It means activity touched that many properties.

For context on the most recent monthly read, May 2026 showed 40,355 properties with filings, down 5% from April but up 14% year over year, at a national monthly rate of one in every 3,562 housing units, per ATTOM's May 2026 report. The month-over-month dip with a year-over-year rise is the pattern of the whole year: bumpy month to month, up over twelve months.

2. Quarterly Trend and What Is Driving It

To make sense of the 2026 numbers you need the 2025 baseline. For full-year 2025, 367,460 U.S. properties had foreclosure filings, up 14% from 2024 and up 3% from 2023, which works out to 0.26% of all U.S. housing units, per ATTOM's year-end 2025 report. That 367,460 figure is a 2025 number and a baseline only, not a 2026 total. I am flagging it because people love to quote it as if it is current.

The history is what keeps this in proportion. REOs in 2025 totaled 46,439, up 27% from 2024, but still down 68% from 2019's 143,955 and down roughly 96% from the 2010 peak of about 1,050,500. So a 27% or even 45% year-over-year jump sounds dramatic until you remember the base it is climbing from is tiny by historical standards.

What is actually driving the rise? ATTOM frames the 2025 to 2026 increase as "continued normalization" of the housing market rather than widespread distress, and points to strong homeowner equity positions as the reason volumes stay low. That tracks. Years of price appreciation left most owners with real cushion, so even when someone falls behind, they usually have enough equity to sell their way out instead of going all the way to repossession. National Mortgage Professional describes the same Q1 jump as pipeline pressure mounting, which is a fair read: the pressure is building, the dam is not breaking.

The honest framing: rising but still low. Anyone selling you a "2008 is back" narrative to push a course or a list product is reading the year-over-year percentage and ignoring the absolute level. The opportunity is real, it is just a steady climb in supply, not a crash. That is good news for a wholesaler who wants a durable lead source rather than a one-time fire sale.

3. Foreclosure Rates by State

National averages hide enormous spread, so this is where the data gets useful. Two different lenses matter here, and conflating them is the most common mistake. Rate per housing unit tells you density, how concentrated distress is in a given state. Raw start volume tells you how many actual doors there are to dial. You want both.

Highest foreclosure rates by state (Q1 2026)

Rank State Foreclosure Rate (Q1 2026)
1 Indiana 1 in 739 housing units
2 South Carolina 1 in 743
3 Florida 1 in 750
4 Delaware 1 in 757
5 Illinois 1 in 833

Those five carry the densest distress in the country for the quarter, with HousingWire corroborating the same top-five ordering including Delaware and Illinois in its coverage of the Q1 2026 data. A state at one in 739 is running close to 40% denser than the national rate of one in 1,211. If you are choosing a small set of counties to test, these states are where a list pull hits the most distressed owners per thousand records.

Highest raw foreclosure start volume by state (Q1 2026)

State Foreclosure Starts (Q1 2026)
Texas 10,617
Florida 10,099
California 7,985
Georgia 4,356
New York 3,886

Notice Florida is the only state in both tables. It has high density and high raw volume, which is why it shows up on nearly every distressed-investor map. Texas and California carry huge raw counts because they are huge states, even though their per-unit rates are nowhere near the worst. For a team running outbound at volume, raw starts matter more than rate, because you need enough records to keep callers busy every day. A dense but small state can run dry fast.

State rankings move month to month. Q1 2026's worst state by rate was Indiana, but in May 2026 the monthly leader was Florida at one in 2,110, followed by South Carolina (1 in 2,287), Maryland (1 in 2,369), Nevada (1 in 2,386), and Indiana (1 in 2,516). Never claim a single "current worst state" without naming the period. The ranking is a snapshot, not a constant.

4. Worst Metros and Fastest-Rising Markets

States are the wide lens. Metros are where you actually build a buybox, because a metro foreclosure rate can run roughly double the national rate even when the state looks average. For the most recent monthly read (May 2026), here are the metros with at least 2 million population that posted the highest foreclosure rates.

Metro (2M+ population) Foreclosure Rate (May 2026)
Cleveland, OH 1 in 1,524 housing units
Baltimore, MD 1 in 1,804
Tampa, FL 1 in 1,878
Riverside, CA 1 in 1,980
Orlando, FL 1 in 2,034

Compare those to the May national monthly rate of one in 3,562 and the point lands. Cleveland at one in 1,524 is running better than 2x the national density. Tampa and Orlando put two Florida metros in the top five, which lines up with Florida leading the state table that same month. Baltimore and Riverside round it out. If you are deciding where to concentrate dials, a metro running 2x the national rate is doing the work of telling you where motivated sellers are clustering.

The practical move is to layer the lenses. Pick a high-rate state, then drill into its highest-rate metros, then build your buybox around those counties. That is how you avoid spreading thin across a whole state when the distress is concentrated in a few pockets. A distressed property in a hot metro is worth more dials than a scattered list across a cold one.

5. Pre-Foreclosure vs Auction vs REO Explained

If you are going to use foreclosure data to find deals, you have to know which stage you are buying into, because the three stages are completely different animals for a wholesaler. Here is the cycle in plain terms.

Stage What It Means Who Controls the Property Wholesaler Fit
Pre-foreclosure / start Lender filed a default notice. The owner is behind but still owns the home. The homeowner Best. Direct seller, real motivation, least competition.
Auction Property is scheduled for a public foreclosure sale. Court / trustee process Harder. Cash and speed required, owner contact window closing.
REO Sale did not produce a buyer, so the bank repossessed it. The bank Crowded. You negotiate with an asset manager, not a motivated person.

A pre-foreclosure is the front of the line. The owner just got served, the clock is running, and they have a real reason to move before the process takes the decision out of their hands. That is the most motivated, least picked-over seller in the whole cycle, which is exactly why the pre-foreclosure lead playbook centers the outreach there. By the time it hits a foreclosure auction, the seller window is closing and you are competing on cash and speed. By REO, the human is gone and you are haggling with a bank.

This is why I keep pointing wholesalers to the starts number. A start is a pre-foreclosure. It is a person, not an institution, and they are at the moment of maximum motivation. The 82,631 starts in Q1 2026 are 82,631 fresh entries into exactly the pool you want to be calling.

6. What Rising Foreclosures Mean for Motivated-Seller Supply

Here is the bridge from data to your pipeline. Foreclosure activity is one of the cleanest public proxies for motivated-seller supply, because a homeowner in default has a reason to sell that does not depend on the market mood. They have a deadline. When starts rise 20% year over year, the pool of deadline-driven sellers is growing at roughly that pace, and that pool sits at the top of your funnel.

But proxy is the key word. Not every distressed owner becomes a deal, and foreclosure lists are not the only motivated-seller source. They sit alongside probate, tired landlords, code violations, and the rest of the motivated-seller lead universe. What the 2026 foreclosure data tells you is directional: where the deadline-driven supply is thickening, and how fast.

The way to use it correctly is to treat rate and volume as a market-selection tool, not a national timing signal. The national rate barely moves your decision. The state and metro spread is what matters. A buybox built around Cleveland, Tampa, or the high-rate Indiana and South Carolina markets is fishing where the fish are. A buybox built on the national average is fishing in the middle of a lake.

The supply read for 2026: growing, concentrated, and early-stage. More owners are entering the funnel (starts up 20%), the growth is concentrated in identifiable states and metros, and most of that new supply is at the pre-foreclosure stage where a wholesaler has the most room to help. That is a workable, durable lead source, not a gold rush.

7. How Wholesalers Should Position for the Wave

So what do you actually do with all this? A few moves, in order.

Pick markets off the spread, not the headline. Use the state rate table to find dense markets, the start-volume table to confirm there are enough doors, and the metro table to drill into the pockets. Indiana, South Carolina, Florida, Delaware, and Illinois lead on density. Texas, Florida, and California lead on raw starts. Where those overlap, like Florida, you get density and volume together.

Pull pre-foreclosure and start lists, not REO lists. You want the owner while they still own the home and still have a reason to talk. That means filtering your list source for default notices and foreclosure starts, then getting on the phone fast before the auction window closes.

Reach them by phone, early and often. A pre-foreclosure owner is not browsing listings, they are stressed and screening calls. Cold calling the start list reaches them at the moment they have a problem and a deadline, which is the whole game. The earlier in the cycle you make contact, the less competition you face and the more of their problem you can actually solve.

Do not try to time the whole country. There is no single month to "go all in" on foreclosures nationally. The supply is steady and concentrated, so the edge comes from picking the right pockets and running consistent outreach into them, month after month, not from waiting for a crash that the equity data says is not coming.

That last point is where most operators lose. Pulling a great distressed list is the easy part. Dialing it every single day, following up on the ones who said "not yet," and keeping the pipeline full while you also try to close deals is what actually breaks people. Rising foreclosure supply only helps you if something is consistently working it.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many foreclosures are happening in 2026 so far?

In Q1 2026, 118,727 properties got a foreclosure filing, up 26% from a year earlier. The most recent monthly read, May 2026, showed 40,355 filings, up 14% year over year. So yeah, it is climbing, but it is a steady rise off low levels, not a 2008-style wave.

Which states have the highest foreclosure rates in 2026?

For Q1 2026, the worst rates were Indiana (1 in 739 housing units), South Carolina (1 in 743), Florida (1 in 750), Delaware (1 in 757), and Illinois (1 in 833). If you are picking a market for distressed deal flow, those are the densest right now.

What is the difference between foreclosure starts and REOs, and which matters for finding deals?

A foreclosure start is when the lender first kicks off the process, so the owner still has the house and a reason to move. An REO is after the bank already took it back. For wholesalers, starts are where the money is, you are reaching a motivated seller early, before it is bank-owned and picked over. Q1 2026 had 82,631 starts nationally, up 20% year over year.

Are foreclosure numbers actually high, or does the news just make it sound that way?

Honestly, they are up but still low by historical standards. The national rate is one in 1,211 housing units for the quarter, and even with the increases, 2025's full-year total of 367,460 was a fraction of the 2010 peak. ATTOM itself calls it normalization, not distress. Strong homeowner equity is keeping a lid on it.

Can I use foreclosure data to decide where to run lead gen?

That is exactly what it is good for. Rate-by-state tells you where distress is concentrated, raw start counts tell you where the volume is. We use signals like this to build a buybox around the markets where motivated sellers are actually showing up, then run the outreach so your pipeline stays full.
