Why SMS Outperforms Most Outbound Channels for Wholesaling

The open rate on SMS is 95% or higher. Email sits at 20-25% on a good day. Cold calls reach maybe 8-15% of numbers dialed. No other outbound channel touches as many eyeballs, per message sent, as a text.

For wholesaling specifically, SMS does something cold calling can't: it reaches sellers who screen calls. A distressed seller with a two-year-old lien on a property they haven't touched isn't picking up a number they don't recognize. But they're reading that text. The channel itself changes who you're able to reach.

Response rates on a well-targeted distressed seller list run 8-12%. On a cold email list to the same audience, you're looking at 1-3%. The conversion difference isn't marginal. When you're running $15-40 cost-per-qualified-response on SMS versus $80-150 on direct mail, the math forces the conversation.

The frame that most operators get wrong: SMS and cold calling are not competing channels. They're complementary. SMS reaches the screeners. Cold calling reaches people who pick up but haven't been texted. Run both, route leads through a single CRM, and you cover the full spectrum of seller behavior.

Speed of response: Sellers who do respond to SMS typically reply within minutes, not hours. That window matters. The operators closing the highest percentages of SMS leads are responding within 5 minutes and getting sellers on the phone same-day.

A2P 10DLC Compliance: What It Is and Why You Can't Skip It

A2P 10DLC stands for Application-to-Person 10-Digit Long Code. It's the FCC registration framework that requires any business sending text messages at scale in the U.S. to register their brand and campaigns with TCR (The Campaign Registry). This is not optional. It's been in effect since 2021 and carriers actively enforce it.

Without registration, your messages hit carrier filters. Deliverability drops to 20-40% of your list. You're paying for messages that never reach the phone. Worse, carriers may start blocking your numbers entirely after repeated unregistered sends. You burn your number pool and have nothing to show for it.

The registration process has two steps: brand registration (who you are as a business) and campaign registration (what you're sending and why). Both go through TCR. Approval takes 2-4 weeks. You cannot start sending at volume before approval comes through. Operators who skip this step and blast anyway are making an expensive mistake they'll discover after their first large campaign lands at 20% deliverability.

Critical timing note: Start your A2P registration 3-4 weeks before you want to run your first campaign. Do not buy a list and wait until registration is approved to start skip tracing. Get both moving in parallel so you're ready to send the day approval comes through.

List Strategy: Who to SMS and Who to Skip

The five list types that produce results for SMS wholesaling campaigns: absentee owners, pre-foreclosure and NOD (Notice of Default), tax delinquent 60+ days, probate, and high-equity vacant properties. Every one of these has a clear motivation signal. You're not texting someone hoping they might want to sell. You're reaching people who have a documented reason to need an exit.

Skip owner-occupied primary residences as your primary SMS target. Motivation is lower, complaint rates are higher, and the conversion math doesn't work. You'll generate opt-outs and potential TCPA exposure without the deal flow to justify it. Focus your list budget on the five categories above.

List freshness matters more for SMS than it does for cold calling. A phone number that's 18 months old on a cold call list might still reach the seller. A phone number that's 18 months old on an SMS list is more likely to belong to someone else now. Stale SMS numbers generate complaints without generating leads. Pull fresh data. For tax delinquent lists specifically, go directly to county records rather than relying on aggregators that update quarterly.

Skip tracing for SMS requires mobile numbers. Landlines don't receive text messages. This seems obvious, but most operators pull a skip trace and accept whatever numbers come back without filtering out landlines and VoIP numbers that won't receive SMS. Filter before you load. Data sources that work: PropStream, BatchLeads, or direct county records. For meaningful volume on a campaign, you need a minimum of 2,000-5,000 records. Below that, your sample size isn't large enough to optimize from.

  • Absentee owners: High equity, non-local, ideal for initial blast
  • Pre-foreclosure/NOD: Time pressure creates urgency. High response to personalized messages.
  • Tax delinquent (60+ days): Financial distress signal. Pull direct from county records for freshness.
  • Probate: Longer sales timeline but motivated sellers. Drip sequences work well here.
  • High-equity vacant: No occupant friction. High likelihood of out-of-state ownership.

Message Templates That Get Responses

The message template that gets responses is short, personal, and direct. No corporate language. No exclamation points. No links in the first message. You are writing a text that looks like it came from a person, because sellers respond to people, not companies.

Keep every message under 160 characters. That's one segment. Once you go over 160 characters, you're at two segments, and your cost doubles. Beyond the cost, longer messages read as bulk marketing. Sellers stop reading after 160 characters anyway.

Two templates that work:

Absentee owner: "Hi [FirstName], I'm a local investor looking to buy property in [city]. Would you consider an offer on [address]? - [Name]"

Tax delinquent: "Hi [FirstName], I saw the property at [address] may be facing some tax issues. I buy properties as-is for cash. Any interest? - [Name]"

What kills response rates: long messages, links in the first touch, referring to yourself as "my company" or "our team" in the opener, multiple questions in a single message, and "CLICK HERE" or any all-caps urgency language. Every one of these signals bulk marketing and drops your response rate. The failure mode here is almost always operators trying to qualify the lead inside the first message instead of simply getting a reply.

One question rule: Your first message should end with exactly one question. Not two. Not a question and a qualifier. One question. More than that and the seller doesn't know what to respond to and doesn't respond at all.

Blast vs. Drip: When to Use Each

A blast sends the same message to your entire list at once. A drip sends an automated sequence of messages over time, triggered by response status or time delays. You need both. Most operators use only one, which is why most operators leave money in the list.

Use blast for: new list acquisition, the first touch on any list, and time-sensitive events (foreclosure deadline approaching, market shift you want to lead with). The blast is your initial contact layer. It's fast, it generates your first wave of responses, and it sets up everything that follows.

Use drip for: following up the non-responders from your blast, re-engaging leads that went cold, and working a list with a longer motivation timeline (probate, for example). A non-response to your initial blast doesn't mean the seller isn't interested. It means they didn't see it, didn't have time, or weren't ready that day. The drip catches them on day 7, day 14, and day 21.

The sequence that produces the most leads: initial blast on day 1, drip message 2 on day 7 (different angle, same list), drip message 3 on day 14, drip message 4 on day 21. Four total touches over three weeks. After touch 4, move unresponsive numbers to a cold re-engagement list and run them quarterly.   • Touch 1 (Day 1): Initial blast. Direct offer inquiry.
  • Touch 2 (Day 7): Different angle. Reference the property specifically. "Just following up on [address]."
  • Touch 3 (Day 14): Light urgency. "Still looking for properties in [area] this month."
  • Touch 4 (Day 21): Final attempt. Keep it simple. "Last message from me on this. Any interest in an offer?"

Combining SMS with Cold Calling

SMS first, call second: blast a list, wait 24-48 hours, then have your cold calling VA work through the non-responders. Opt-Out Handling and Legal Requirements

Every single message you send must include opt-out language. The standard: "Reply STOP to opt out." This isn't a best practice. It's a legal requirement under TCPA. Carriers also require it as part of A2P registration compliance. Messages that don't include opt-out language get flagged and can result in your campaigns being suspended.

When someone opts out, you have 10 business days to process the removal under federal requirements. In practice, your platform should handle this automatically and immediately. Never wait 10 days. Process opt-outs as they come in. VA Horizon's GHL setup handles opt-out processing automatically. The number gets removed from active sequences and added to the suppression list without manual intervention.

The suppression list is permanent. Build it from day one and scrub against it before every campaign. This means before you upload a new list to your SMS platform, you filter out any numbers that have previously opted out from any of your campaigns. Most operators forget this step when they bring in fresh skip-traced data on a new list. They re-text opted-out numbers and trigger TCPA exposure on a contact that already told them to stop.

TCPA violations run $500 to $1,500 per violation. These compound fast if you're re-texting an opt-out list at scale. The operators who've faced TCPA litigation almost always made the same mistake: they moved fast, ignored the suppression list, and texted people who had explicitly opted out on a previous campaign. The compliance infrastructure is not overhead. It's what keeps your operation running.

Timing: Best Days and Hours

Best days to send: Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday. Monday is high-noise for most sellers. Friday afternoons the attention has already shifted to the weekend. Saturday and Sunday generate higher complaint rates and deliver worse conversion even when messages go through.

Best hours: 9 AM to 11 AM local time, or 5 PM to 7 PM local time. Mid-day sends (12 PM to 2 PM) are mediocre. Late morning hits sellers before lunch. Evening hits sellers after work when they're more relaxed and less likely to be dismissive. Both windows produce significantly better response rates than mid-afternoon.

Never send before 8 AM or after 9 PM local time. This is the Quiet Hours requirement under TCPA. Violations here are automatic liability. If your list covers multiple time zones, your platform needs to account for local time. A 9 AM eastern blast lands at 6 AM Pacific. That's a TCPA violation for every number in the western half of your list.

Volume pacing: don't blast 5,000 messages at exactly 9:00 AM. Stagger over 2-3 hours. Carrier throttling is a real problem at high volumes. Sending in bursts instead of evenly distributed sends increases your throttle risk, which drops deliverability on exactly the campaign you spent the most setting up. Spread the send window and protect your deliverability rate.

  • Best days: Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday
  • Best morning window: 9 AM - 11 AM local time
  • Best evening window: 5 PM - 7 PM local time
  • Hard stop: Never before 8 AM or after 9 PM local time (TCPA Quiet Hours)
  • Avoid: Saturday morning (high complaint rate), Sunday (TCPA gray area)

What to Do When a Lead Responds

Speed is the variable most operators underestimate. When a seller responds to your SMS, respond within 5 minutes. After 30 minutes, response rates to your follow-up drop by 60%. Sellers who text back are in an action-oriented moment. That window is short. Miss it and you're chasing a conversation that's already cooled.

Your first response goal is one thing: get them on the phone. Not qualify them over text. Not ask four questions. Get them on the phone. The faster you move from text exchange to voice conversation, the higher your appointment rate. Template: "Thanks for responding. Are you available for a quick 5-minute call today? What time works?" That's the entire message. Send it within 5 minutes of their reply.

When a seller won't call: don't abandon the lead. Qualify via text. Ask: timeline (when are you looking to sell?), condition (is the property in good shape or does it need work?), and what they're looking for (cash offer, specific price?). These three questions give your acquisition manager enough to make an offer. But do not give a price over text. Get them on the phone for that conversation. Pricing over text creates misaligned expectations and kills deals before they start.

Hot leads (seller says yes to a call, confirms timeline, indicates urgency) go to your acquisition manager immediately for a same-day call. Not tomorrow. Same day. The failure mode here is almost always a seller who was ready to talk on Tuesday and got a call Thursday. By then they've listed with an agent or had second thoughts. Your AM's job is to catch hot leads at peak motivation.

Measuring Results: Metrics That Matter

Response rate is your primary campaign health metric. On a fresh, targeted list (absentee owners, NOD, tax delinquent) a solid response rate is 5-12%. Below 2% tells you one of two things: list quality problem or a compliance issue dropping deliverability. Both are fixable, but you need to know which one it is before you send another campaign.

Cost per response: total campaign cost divided by total responses. Target under $50. If you're above $50 per response, either your message template isn't generating replies or your list quality is poor. Run a split test on the message template first. Cheaper fix, faster feedback loop. If response rate holds below 5% after template optimization, the list is the problem.

Appointment rate from responses: 15-30% of responses should convert to an appointment. If your rate is below 15%, the issue is almost always response speed or follow-up execution. You're not losing those leads because they weren't interested. You're losing them because someone responded and waited 2 hours for a reply. That's an operations problem, not a marketing problem.

Opt-out rate is your compliance signal. Above 3% opt-out rate is a red flag. It means your list is stale (numbers have changed hands), your messaging is too aggressive, or you're texting the wrong list type. Address it immediately. A 5%+ opt-out rate is a carrier red flag that can trigger campaign suspension.

  • Response rate target: 5-12% on a targeted list. Below 2% means investigate immediately.
  • Cost per response target: Under $50. Above $50 means template or list quality issue.
  • Appointment rate target: 15-30% of responses. Below 15% is a response speed problem.
  • Opt-out rate limit: Under 3%. Above 3% is a list quality or messaging red flag.
  • Track separately: Initial blast response rate vs. drip sequence response rate. Drip rates should improve as messaging is refined.
Reporting cadence: Pull these numbers after every campaign and after each drip touch. If you're not reviewing metrics by campaign and by list type, you can't optimize.
