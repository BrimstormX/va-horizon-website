## Post 4 — Tactical breakdown
**Format:** Tactical | **Topic:** HighLevel CRM | **Day:** Thu

If your HighLevel pipeline only has Lead, Hot, and Closed stages, you are losing 30 to 40 percent of your follow-up leads.

Here is the structure that stops the leak:

- New Lead (under 24 hours old)
- Qualified, no offer made
- Offer made, awaiting response
- Negotiation
- Verbal agreement
- Contract sent
- Contract signed
- Dispo
- Closed

Each stage gets its own automation. New Leads trigger an SMS within 5 minutes. Qualified leads get a daily follow-up reminder. Offer Made gets a 3-day check-in.

The reason most pipelines fail is not the CRM. It is that the operator never decided what each stage means and what should happen when a deal sits there for too long.

A wholesaler in Tampa cut his dispo time from 21 days to 6 days last quarter. Same buyer's list. No new buyers added.

## Post 16 — Tactical breakdown
**Format:** Tactical | **Topic:** Hiring | **Day:** Thu

How to interview a real estate cold calling VA in 25 minutes:

1. (3 min) Ask them to define MAO and ARV. If they hesitate, end.

2. (5 min) Live roleplay. You are the seller, they are the caller. Pick a hostile opener. "How did you get my number?"

3. (5 min) Show them a real HighLevel screenshot. Ask them to walk you through what they would do with a lead in the New stage.

4. (5 min) Ask for their dial count from their last gig. Anything under 500 per day on a predictive dialer is a red flag.

5. (5 min) Ask why they left their last 2 wholesaling jobs. Listen for blame. Blame on the operator means turnover risk.

2 minutes for questions from them. 1 minute for next steps.

If you are running this interview more than 4 times to find one good VA, your job posting is the problem, not the candidate pool.

## Post 22 — Tactical breakdown
**Format:** Tactical | **Topic:** Cold calling scripts | **Day:** Thu

The opener that converts cold calls into appointments at twice the rate of the standard "Hi, I was wondering if you would consider selling":

"Hi [first name], this is [VA name]. I'm calling about the property at [address]. I was wondering if you've ever thought about selling it."

Three things changed:

1. Use their first name, not "sir" or "ma'am". Sounds local.
2. Reference the specific address. Sounds like you researched, not blasted.
3. Past tense on "thought about". Removes the active commitment ask. They can say "yeah I've thought about it" without agreeing to anything.

Tested across 12,000 calls last quarter. Appointment rate went from 1.1 percent to 2.4 percent on the same list.

## Post 32 — Medium insight
**Format:** Medium insight | **Topic:** CRM | **Day:** Thu

The reason your sales pipeline reports lie to you:

Stages in HighLevel do not advance unless someone moves the card manually. If your VA forgets to move a lead from "Qualified" to "Offer Made" after sending an offer, your report says you have not made offers.

The fix is automation. Trigger the stage move on the action that should advance the stage:

- SMS sent with offer language? Auto-advance to "Offer Made"
- Contract template sent? Auto-advance to "Contract Sent"
- Calendar booking created? Auto-advance to "Meeting Scheduled"

Cards still move manually for human-judgment advances. But the mechanical ones are automated.

Reports start telling the truth. Decisions improve.

## Post 36 — Tactical breakdown
**Format:** Tactical | **Topic:** SMS | **Day:** Thu

The 4-message SMS sequence that re-engages a cold list better than a phone blast:

Day 1: "Hi [name], are you the owner of [address]?"
Day 3 (if no reply): "Just wanted to follow up. The address came up on a list I'm working with. No pressure."
Day 7 (if no reply): "One more try. If you would consider a cash offer, I have a buyer interested. If not, I'll leave you alone."
Day 14 (if no reply): "Hi [name], last note. Marking this as a no for now. Feel free to reach out if anything changes."

Response rate compounding across the sequence: 11 to 14 percent on a cold list.

The Day 14 message is the surprise driver. Sellers reply to confirm "yes, no", which gives you permission to actually close them out or push.
