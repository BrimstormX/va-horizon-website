How To Use This Tool

How to use the Wholesale Deal ROI Calculator

The calculator takes the same numbers a wholesaler tracks every week (records dialed, contact rate, qualified rate, contract rate, average assignment fee, and monthly VA spend) and projects steady-state output. It is not a forecast for month one. It is a model for what your operation produces once the VA is ramped, the list is dialed in, and the CRM is filling with usable follow-ups.

Pull the inputs from your CRM or dialer reports if you have them. If you are pre-launch, the defaults represent a typical VA Horizon engagement: 8,000 records per month, 10% contact, 6% qualified, 8% contract conversion, $12,000 average assignment, and $1,160 monthly cost for one cold calling VA plus the Readymode dialer. Move the sliders to stress test what happens when contact rate drops to 6%, or what your numbers look like with a $20,000 average assignment fee.

What is a realistic contact rate for cold calling wholesalers?

Contact rate is the single biggest variable in this model. On a predictive dialer like Readymode running at 800 to 1,000 dials per 8-hour shift, most operations land between 8% and 15% connection rate. The wide range is driven by three things: list freshness (older lists with stale numbers connect less), skip tracing quality (cell numbers connect 2x more often than landlines), and the time of day the VA is dialing (between 9 AM and 11 AM local plus 5 PM to 7 PM local pull the highest answer rates).

Manual single-line dialing pushes contact rates higher, often 20% to 30%, but you pay for it in volume. A single-line manual caller hits 150 to 250 dials per shift instead of 800 to 1,000. The math almost always favors a predictive dialer for outbound wholesaling because you need volume to surface motivated sellers, and motivated sellers are rare on any list.

If your contact rate sits below 6%, the problem is usually the list, not the VA. Run a fresh skip trace, scrub disconnects, and rotate to a different vertical of distressed lists (absentee owners with high equity, pre-foreclosure, tax delinquent 60+ days).

How to interpret your qualified lead rate

A qualified lead is a seller who has timeline (typically 90 days or less), motivation (financial pressure, vacant property, inherited property, divorce, or job relocation), and is open to a cash offer at a discount. Healthy qualified rates on connections run 4% to 8%. Below 3%, you have a list problem, a script problem, or both. Above 10% usually means the bar for qualification is set too low and your acquisition manager is wasting time on leads that will never close.

VA Horizon trains every cold calling VA on a wholesaling-specific qualification framework. We do not let VAs mark leads as qualified just because the seller said maybe. The lead needs concrete timeline plus motivation plus openness to discount before it gets tagged in the CRM for acquisition manager handoff.

What is a healthy contract conversion rate?

Contract rate (qualified leads that go to contract) is where most wholesalers leak deals. The healthy range is 5% to 12%. Speed to lead is the single biggest driver. A qualified lead contacted within 5 minutes converts at roughly 2x the rate of the same lead contacted 30 minutes later. After 2 hours, conversion drops 70% or more from the 5-minute baseline.

The other big driver is the acquisition manager. An experienced AM running 25 to 40 qualified leads per week with a tight follow-up cadence will close 8% to 12%. A part-time AM juggling leads without a CRM-enforced cadence usually lands at 3% to 6%, sometimes lower. This is why VA Horizon places dedicated Acquisition Managers at $1,440/month for clients who do not have one in-house.

Reading your ROI multiple

ROI multiple is monthly revenue divided by monthly VA cost. Under 3x, you have a serious problem somewhere: list quality, VA performance, follow-up speed, or assignment fee size. Between 5x and 15x is the typical healthy range for a single VA engagement. Above 20x usually means either your list is unusually productive (large pre-foreclosure pipeline in a tight market) or your assignment fees are above market norms.

Do not optimize for the highest ROI multiple. Optimize for total net dollars. A wholesaler doing 4x ROI on three VAs at $1,000 each ($12,000 net) makes more money than a wholesaler doing 12x ROI on one VA ($12,920 gross minus other operational costs). Scale beats efficiency once unit economics are positive.

Cost per qualified lead as a sanity check

Cost per qualified lead (monthly cost divided by qualified leads produced) is the cleanest single number for comparing your operation against alternatives. VA Horizon engagements typically land at $20 to $40 per qualified lead. A freelance VA without QA runs $30 to $60. Pay-per-lead services run $75 to $250 per qualified lead and rarely deliver the same quality of qualification (most pay-per-lead services count anyone with a property as a lead).

If your CPQL is over $60 with a managed VA, something in the funnel is leaking. Check VA dial volume first (target 800+ per shift), then list quality, then qualification standards. Most of the time, the fix is list refresh, not staffing change.
